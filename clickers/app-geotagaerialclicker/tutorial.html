Namibia Wildlife Challenge Tutorial

Thanks for joining this digital expedition to Namibia!



Rangers at Kuzikus Wildlife Reserve need your help to find wild animals in their reserve. This will enable our ranger friends to better protect these beautiful animals from poachers and other threats.




According to the rangers, "Rhino poaching continues to be a growing problem that threatens to extinguish some rhino species within a decade or two. Rhino monitoring is thus important for their protection. Using digital maps in combination with MicroMappers to trace aerial images of rhinos could greatly improve rhino monitoring efforts."




This is where we come in!


Our shields strategy has changed, so please read carefully.

We'll use the "Aerial Clicker" to search for wildlife in aerial photographs of the Kuzikus Reserve. In this mini tutorial, we'll show you how to draw "digital shields" around the animals you find. But first, here are examples of helpful shields (versus unhelpful ones):




The white outlines are the shields that we drew using the Aerial Clicker above. Notice that our shields include the shadows of the animals, this important. If the animals are close to each other, the shields can overlap but there can only be one shield per animal (one shield per rhino in this case : )

Some final tips before your expedition!

  • If you create a profile (click on Community above), you'll be able to keep track of your clicks and accumulate do-good points.
  • Note that you can use the "+" and "-" buttons to zoom in for a closer look and to pan out.
  • Don't worry if you don't see many animals! Knowing where there are no animals is just as important to the rangers in Namibia. If you're not sure whether what you see is an animal, draw a shield anyway just in case!
  • If you have any questions during the expedition or simply want to hang out with fellow digital rangers, we have our own chat room here.
  • This expedition has two goals: (1) to look for wild animals, and (2) to test this new "Aerial Clicker" before we actually use it in response to a humanitarian disaster. This means that the tool is not perfect. We're doing this pilot so we can test and improve the Clicker. So thank you very much for your patience if the Clicker does not (yet) work as well as we'd like. Your feedback is super important for future humanitarian applications, so feel free to share your suggestions here.
  • If you are an anonymous user, you will not get any credit for your contributions. Sign in now!
  • And now, lets go find wild animals in Namibia!
